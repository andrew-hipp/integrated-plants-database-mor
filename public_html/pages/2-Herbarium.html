What is an herbarium?

An herbarium is a library of dried plants. Plants are mounted on archival-quality paper, with a label that provides information on where, when, and by whom the plant was collected. Collectors provide detailed locality and habitat information, along with a complete plant specimen. Carefully prepared and handled specimens will last for hundreds of years. The herbarium thus serves as a biodiversity time capsule, and one of our most important tools for studying how the world's biodiversity has changed over time.

The Morton Arboretum's herbarium houses more than 169,000 dried plant specimens and 19,000 lichens. Our herbarium's strongest collections are its (1) active and nearly comprehensive contemporary collection of the flora of the 24-county Chicago region and (2) broad collection of woody plants of the northern Temperate zone, both cultivated and spontaneous. These two core collections are databased nearly in their entirety. In this online portal, you can explore the fascinating flora of our region and the changing landscape of the Arboretum's living collections.

What can you do with an herbarium?

Herbarium data allow us to pinpoint where plants have grown, and when they were there. The data available to you in this portal and through other herbaria worldwide can be used in many ways. Here are just three of the many ways that you might use the herbarium and the data presented in this database:

  • Discover new species. Scientists estimate that there are perhaps 70,000 flowering plant species unknown to science, and that more than half of those may be lurking in the world's herbaria. Herbarium data on plant distributions and habitats, and careful study of the plant specimens themselves, are crucial in understanding the scope of plant biodiversity worldwide.
  • Learn to identify plants. Herbaria provide a large and often well studied collection of species. Scans of many of our specimens are available online (look for the camera icon: ). In a recent study, researchers found that access to herbaria significantly improves botanists' ability to correctly identify plants, and contributes to a better understanding of local plant biodiversity.
  • Study how global climate change affects plant ranges and life history. As our climate warms and patterns of precipitation change, plant distributions are changing as well. How will this affect what our world looks like in 50 years? Herbaria provide a means to look back into the recent past, and they reveal that plants have responded quite rapidly to climate change.

If you are intrigued by what one can do with an herbarium, you might also enjoy this list of more than 70 uses for herbaria compiled by an herbarium colleague. In the meantime, we hope you enjoy learning more about plants through your studies in the Arboretum's online database.



Herbarium contacts and additional information

The herbarium depends on our more than 30 active volunteers, who collect plants, mount specimens, file and accession, make labels, enter data, develop databases and aid in nomenclatural research, and assist in many other tasks.
